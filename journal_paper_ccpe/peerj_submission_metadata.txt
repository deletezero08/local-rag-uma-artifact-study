Title
An Artifact-Backed Empirical Study of Local RAG Execution on Constrained Unified Memory Devices

Abstract
Local Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) on Apple Silicon Unified Memory Architecture (UMA) is constrained not only by retrieval quality, but by the shared execution budget consumed by model weights, KV cache, retrieved context, and operating-system activity. This paper studies a local RAG execution path that combines GGUF deployment, TurboQuant retrieval compression, and KV-aware context pruning on a Mac mini M4 with 16GB unified memory. Within the Falcon-7B family, migrating from transformers + MPS to a quantized GGUF route reduces TTFT from 77.6 s to 123.7 ms; across deployable configurations, Llama-3-8B-Q + GGUF serves as the preferred practical reference at 51.2 ms. In the keep-ratio scan, keep_ratio = 0.6 reduces TTFT from 5.83 s to 0.87 s while decode throughput remains in the 18--19 TPS range. Under concurrent load, the optimized path lowers P95 TTFT and wall time, while also exposing a stability cost at higher concurrency. Stage-wise latency decomposition shows that prompt-side and retrieval-side optimizations recover deployability on the tested device, but the dominant residual cost migrates to generation, especially decode. These findings are reported as an artifact-linked systems study on a constrained UMA deployment path and support the view that local RAG optimization is best treated as shared-budget systems co-design rather than isolated module acceleration.
